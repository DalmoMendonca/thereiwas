Google Timeline has years of my location data. It can tell me where I went, but it does very little with the memory. I wanted one button: Play.

This is There I Was. I can import a standard Timeline JSON file, or try three real trips from my own export. I will open California.

This trip starts in Oklahoma, reaches the West Coast, and returns home. Timeline often records only scattered points, so There I Was preserves detailed paths and uses Mapbox Directions to rebuild sparse drives along actual roads. The map fits the complete 3,030-mile loop, not a cloud of disconnected dots.

Now GPT-5.6 does something the map cannot do alone. Direct replay sends a small dossier of named places, dates, travel modes, and summarized movement. It receives no raw coordinates, home location, source file, or photos.

The model chooses the trip's chapters, sets their pacing, and writes grounded captions. Those timestamps change the playback itself. This is not text sitting beside the product. GPT-5.6 is directing the product.

Press Play memory. The route follows the model's pacing, captions appear at the right moments, and the full loop stays visible. I can also add geotagged photos; their time and location are read locally and synchronized to the replay.

When the replay ends, it leaves me with something. There I Was renders the route, dates, summary, chapters, and trip statistics into a 1080 by 1350 memory card. It is made in the browser, and I can save or share it without uploading my Timeline.

Google collected the record. There I Was turns it into a memory I can replay and keep.
